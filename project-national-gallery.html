See Me See You Exhibition Microsite

Role Web Developer
Client National Gallery Singapore
Agency Do Not Design
Timeline Feb 2023 - Oct 2023
View Live Site (Might be unactive)

Overview

Developed an interactive companion website for the National Gallery Singapore's "See Me See You" exhibition, collaborating with creative agency Do Not Design. The microsite serves as a digital companion to the physical exhibition, providing visitors with a rich online resource featuring a visual timeline of artworks and in-depth information about each piece.

This project represents the intersection of art, technology, and digital storytelling—creating an accessible platform that enhances the gallery experience and makes the exhibition's narrative available to audiences beyond the physical space.

Project Context

The National Gallery Singapore is Southeast Asia's premier art museum, housing the world's largest public collection of Singapore and Southeast Asian art. For their "See Me See You" exhibition, they wanted a digital experience that would:

  • Complement the physical exhibition with an interactive online presence
  • Engage younger, tech-savvy audiences with innovative web experiences
  • Extend the exhibition's reach to international audiences unable to visit in person

The Challenge

Creating an engaging digital companion for a physical art exhibition presented several unique challenges:

  • Designing an intuitive timeline interface to showcase the exhibition's artworks chronologically
  • Presenting detailed artwork information in an engaging, accessible way
  • Creating smooth 3D visual elements and interactions without overwhelming the content
  • Balancing visual richness with performance and load times
  • Ensuring the experience worked seamlessly on both desktop and mobile devices

The Solution

Built an engaging, interactive companion website using Three.js for visual enhancements, creating a seamless digital experience:

  • Interactive Timeline: Visual timeline showcasing the exhibition's artworks chronologically, allowing users to explore the narrative flow of the exhibition
  • Detailed Artwork Pages: In-depth information for each art piece including artist backgrounds, historical context, themes, and curatorial insights
  • 3D Visual Elements: Used Three.js to create engaging visual effects and transitions that enhance the browsing experience without distracting from content
  • Responsive Design: Optimized experience for desktop, tablet, and mobile devices with adaptive layouts
  • Performance Optimization: Fast loading times while maintaining rich visual presentation

"Digital companions should enrich understanding and appreciation of art, making complex narratives accessible to all."

Technical Implementation

As a front-end-only companion website, the project focused on creating an engaging, informative experience with thoughtful use of 3D enhancements:

Artwork Information Pages

  • Detailed pages for each artwork with high-quality images
  • Structured content including artist bios, historical context, and themes
  • Clean typography and layout prioritizing readability
  • Easy navigation between artworks and back to timeline

Three.js Visual Enhancements

  • Subtle 3D effects and transitions adding visual interest without overwhelming content
  • Performance-optimized rendering maintaining smooth interactions
  • WebGL effects that degrade gracefully on older devices

Performance & Accessibility

  • Fast initial load times with progressive enhancement
  • Responsive images optimized for different screen sizes
  • Semantic HTML structure for screen reader compatibility

Key Features

  1. Interactive Timeline – Chronological visualization of exhibition artworks, allowing users to explore the narrative progression of the show
  2. Comprehensive Artwork Information – Detailed pages for each piece featuring artist backgrounds, historical context, themes, and curatorial insights
  3. Three.js Visual Enhancements – Subtle 3D effects and animations that add polish without distracting from the content
  4. Multi-Device Support – Fully responsive design working seamlessly on desktop, tablet, and mobile
  5. Fast, Accessible Experience – Quick loading times with semantic HTML and keyboard navigation support

Collaboration with Do Not Design

Working with Do Not Design, a creative agency, involved close collaboration throughout the development process:

  • Design Implementation: Translated creative vision into functional 3D experiences, working iteratively with designers
  • Technical Consultation: Advised on technical feasibility and performance implications of design choices
  • Stakeholder Communication: Regular presentations to National Gallery stakeholders, incorporating feedback
  • Quality Assurance: Rigorous testing across devices and browsers to ensure consistent experience

Challenges & Solutions

Challenge: Creating an engaging timeline interface that works well on both desktop and mobile devices with different interaction patterns.

Solution: Implemented adaptive timeline controls—horizontal scrolling with mouse drag on desktop, vertical scroll with touch gestures on mobile. Added clear visual indicators for navigation and maintained smooth performance across both interfaces.

Challenge: Balancing rich visual effects with fast load times and smooth performance, especially on mobile devices.

Solution: Used progressive enhancement approach—core content loads immediately with basic styling, then Three.js effects load in the background. Implemented efficient asset loading and optimized images. Result: sub-2-second initial load with smooth 60fps animations.

Results & Impact

  • Successfully launched alongside physical exhibition in October 2023
  • Featured on National Gallery Singapore's main website as official exhibition companion
  • Sub-5-second initial load time with smooth 60fps animations
  • Site remained live through early 2024, serving as lasting digital archive of the exhibition

Learnings

This project taught me the importance of restraint when using advanced technologies like Three.js. The 3D effects served to enhance the content, not overshadow it. Knowing when to use subtle enhancements versus when to let content speak for itself is a crucial skill.

Working with a cultural institution like the National Gallery showed me how to create digital experiences that serve educational and curatorial goals. The timeline and detailed artwork information needed to be both engaging and informative—technology was a tool to make cultural content more accessible, not an end in itself.

Collaborating with Do Not Design taught me to bridge creative vision with technical implementation. Translating design concepts like "elegant transitions" and "engaging timeline" into concrete technical decisions—animation timings, scroll behaviors, information architecture—requires clear communication and iterative refinement.
